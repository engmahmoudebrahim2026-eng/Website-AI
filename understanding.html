Core Definition

What Exactly Is AI?

Artificial Intelligence is a branch of computer science focused on building systems that perform tasks requiring human intelligence: reasoning, learning, understanding language, recognizing patterns.

The term was coined at the Dartmouth Conference in 1956 by John McCarthy. Unlike traditional software with fixed rules, AI systems learn from data and adapt.

The result: systems that handle situations they were never explicitly programmed for — and improve with every interaction.

Classification

Three Types of AI

Exists Today🔵

Narrow AI (ANI)

Specialized in one task. Cannot transfer skills to other domains. Despite its name, it dramatically outperforms humans in its area.

Subject of intense AI safety research and debate among the world's leading scientists.

Core Technology

Machine Learning (ML)

ML is the engine behind most AI. Instead of explicit rules, systems learn patterns directly from data and improve automatically with experience.

📊
Supervised Learning

Trains on labeled input-output pairs. Used for spam detection, image classification, medical diagnosis.

🔍
Unsupervised Learning

Finds hidden structure in unlabeled data. Powers customer segmentation and recommendation systems.

🎮
Reinforcement Learning

Learns via rewards and penalties through trial and error. How AlphaGo mastered Go and robots learn to walk.

Advanced Layer

Deep Learning & Neural Networks

Deep Learning uses neural networks with many hidden layers inspired by the human brain. Each layer extracts higher-level features progressively.

Example: A face network detects edges → shapes → facial parts → complete faces. This hierarchy gives deep learning its power.

Essential AI Glossary

Algorithm

Step-by-step rules a computer follows to solve a problem.

Neural Network

Computational model with interconnected layers inspired by the brain.

Training Data

Dataset used to teach an AI by showing examples of inputs and outputs.

Transformer

Neural network architecture (Google, 2017) powering all modern LLMs.

Hallucination

When AI confidently generates false but plausible-sounding information.

Overfitting

When a model memorizes training data, failing to generalize to new data.

Prompt Engineering

Crafting AI inputs carefully to get better, more accurate outputs.

Fine-Tuning

Adapting a pre-trained model to a specific task with a smaller dataset.

AI Bias

Systematic errors from flawed assumptions or unrepresentative training data.
